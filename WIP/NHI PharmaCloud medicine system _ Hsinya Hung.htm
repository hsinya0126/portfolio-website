    NHI PharmaCloud Medicine System Design

    Designing a digitized medicine system for the Taiwanese government to facilitate patients’ drug safety and reduce duplicate prescriptions.

    TYPE

    Evaluative Research

    Information Arcgitecture

    Healthcare

    TIMELINE

    Jan. 2020 - Apr. 2020

    CLIENT

    National Health Insurance Administration, Ministry of Health and Welfare, Taiwan

    ROLE

    Lead UX Researcher, Project Manager

    TEAM

    3 UX Researchers, 2 Designers

    Introduction

    Taiwan is one of the countries in the world that provide quality national health insurance. Due to these cheap and reachable resources, people prefer medical institutions rather than pharmacies, causing short clinical sessions, doctors highly need a real-time system to inquiry into the latest prescriptions in order to facilitate patients’ drug safety and reduce duplicate prescriptions. However, though the National Health Insurance (NHI) PharmaCloud medicine system has already existed for years, there is still a lack of useful interface and smooth user flow. Inefficiencies in session tasks limit doctors’ and pharmacists’ ability to provide better prescriptions in a comparably short time.

    ​

    As the lead UX researcher, I conducted mixed-method research to help the Ministry of Health and Welfare redesign the user experience and interface of the NHI PharmaCloud medicine system. I aimed to increase the efficiency of doctors' working routines, enhance the transparency of medical records, and increase patient drug safety. With the new design, doctors could manage the flow of patient data smoothly and seamlessly, ease their physical and mental burden during clinical sessions, and share information with patients to improve their relationship with them. The final design successfully increased overall satisfaction and usability by 25% among healthcare professionals.

    Process

    In order to fully understand the system and design the ideal interface, I decided to put much time into the research stage and applied mixed research methodologies. I led our team to conduct field studies, interviews, card sortings, heuristic evaluation, and usability testings to collect enough information for UX strategies and successfully shift the outcome system back to the government.

    💡 Research Stage

    To better understand the clinical field and what are the main tasks our users have, I led the team to visit the clinic and hospital to conduct field studies and interviews. Also, we spent much time studying the manual of the NHI system and its history file to reduce the huge professional medical knowledge gap. After collecting all the data, I built out a journey map to sort out the general tasks and difficulties of the main users, doctors and pharmacists.

    💡 Evaluation Stage

    During the initial research phase, we conducted semi-structured interviews and observations with stakeholders, which included 1 doctor, 1 engineer, and 1 system manager. This allowed us to become familiar with system functions, gain insights into medical situations, and obtain an in-depth understanding of the clinical context and current working practices. In addition to this, we reviewed the manual guidebook and the system's backstage behavioral data of users to gain insights into the user flow, identify strengths and weaknesses of the current solutions, and determine if they can be incorporated in this particular context.

     

    For the heuristic evaluation, we invited 3 design experts and professors to provide feedback separately in the first section, and then we accumulated their advice to utilize it in the second section of the evaluation. This process revealed 93 usability problems which were classified into three categories: UX catastrophe, Major UX problem, and Minor UX problem. We also proposed design solutions accordingly. It was interesting to note that most of the problems (42 items) were related to the consistency and standards of the system. Additionally, we carried out 20 usability tests, card sortings, user tests, and interviews involving healthcare professionals such as doctors and pharmacists to identify individual usability problems and iterated on our prototype designs.

    Usability Testings & Card Sortings Results

    Analyzed 20 usability testing results and the success rate of each task.

    There are 11 important types of information in the system: Western medicine, traditional Chinese medicine medication records, records and results from laboratory tests and exams, detailed surgical data, dental dispositions and surgical records, allergy medication records, specific controlled drug administration records, specific coagulation factor administration records, discharge summaries, and CDC preventive inoculations.

    Key data from usability testing and card sortings are listed above. Based on the data of correlation, sequence-of-use ranking of different types of information, and completion rates of each information task, we could rearrange the information structure and sort out the use cases. With testing data, we can view the overall system from a more comprehensive perspective and keep an eye on the hierarchical information and connections between multiple types of information.

    💡 Triangulated Findings

    Findings 1

    Significant needs to prioritize and locate the data quickly

    Doctors have to perform many tasks in a specific sequence and under time pressure: insert the patient’s identity card, check physical condition, review individual data, provide feedback, diagnose the disease, give a prescription, and prepare for the next patient. The heavy multitasking and information overload for clinicians restricts the clinic's ability to expand its services while ensuring quality for more patients in need. A digital solution should make the process more efficient and help prevent errors.

    ​

    Findings 2

    Information specifics vary by the division of clinicians

    The specifics of information vary significantly depending on the division and treatment of clinicians, such as dentists and Chinese medicine doctors. This makes it difficult to standardize data collection into a single rubric or a single page. Therefore, customizing data collection is critical to accommodate clinicians' use cases. Clinic situations also vary dramatically. Clinicians need highly flexible tables to conveniently acquire the data they need.

    ​

    Findings 3

    Significance of access and privacy when acquiring patients' information

    Efficient access to necessary information is critical. However, due to information silos, clinicians may encounter difficulties when using the real-time system to inquire about the latest prescriptions. Additionally, clinicians have to log into the system to access patients' personal health information and log out for each clinic section, as preventing the leaking of private data to others is crucial. It is easy for clinicians to forget to log out between session breaks and inadvertently reveal data to others in the clinic.

    ​

    Findings 4

    Existing information gap between doctors' and patient's progress
    In clinical settings, communication between clinicians and patients mainly occurs during debriefing sessions. However, most updates on patient progress are conveyed verbally, which can be frustrating due to the use of jargon and complicated measurements or criteria. Additionally, despite patients in the intensive program visiting the doctor frequently, each clinical session typically only allows for about five minutes, which is not enough time for patients to fully convey their understanding.

    💡 Results & Impact

    With a SUS score of 84.5, our new design demonstrated a 26% increase in usability. Our strategic design and research efforts were pivotal in convincing the government to invest in a major overhaul of the system, leading to a more integrated and effective experience on both mobile and laptop platforms. This improvement received positive responses from doctors and other healthcare professionals, underscoring the success of the project.
